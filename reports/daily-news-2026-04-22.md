Along with the funding, the company said it will open ‌an office in the US state of Arizona, ‌near ‌the manufacturing facilities of Intel and Taiwan Semiconductor ‌Manufacturing Co, and that former Intel CEO ⁠Pat Gelsinger will be joining its board of directors. Modern AI chips such as those from Nvidia, Google and others are not a single chip, but rather multiple chips bonded together with what is ​known as advanced packaging technology, mostly from TSMC. Packaging has become a bottleneck for major chip designers. The basic concept ⁠behind most advanced packaging techniques is to arrange chips together on top of a base layer that connects them all together. That base layer is currently manufactured somewhat like a very large chip in its own right, making it expensive and time-consuming to build. Syenta takes a different approach, creating what CEO and co-founder Jekaterina Viktorova said is somewhat like a stamp that electrochemically transfers the copper wiring needed ​onto the base layer with 40 percent fewer ⁠steps and without using any unusual manufacturing tools. ⁠ The result is that many more base layers can be made per day. "This process takes ​minutes, as opposed to several hours, so it's a massive difference in ‌how you build ⁠your copper interconnects," Viktorova said in an interview. Gelsinger, now a venture capitalist at Playground Global, a Silicon Valley firm that led the financing round, said that in addition ‌to faster manufacturing, Syenta's technology would also help speed up the connections on AI chips. "You open up a much bigger, more standardized, more available supply chain, yet with the density and performance" gains ​that drove chipmakers toward such complex designs in the first place, Gelsinger said. Viktorova said Syenta is working with several chip designers and targeting high-volume production by 2028. "You open up a much bigger, more standardized, more available supply chain, yet with the density and performance" gains ​that drove chipmakers toward such complex designs in the first place, Gelsinger said. Viktorova said Syenta is working with several chip designers and targeting high-volume production by 2028. It became a discussion about how people actually use the tools in front of them. Huang’s argument focused on access. The tools themselves are not the issue. The way people interact with them is what is changing. “These tools now have a brand new user interface,” said Huang. “This new user interface is called artificial intelligence… agentic systems that allow us to now re-express how we use the tools.” For decades, creative software has relied on a familiar model. Users learn where things are, build up shortcuts and operate within the boundaries of the interface. Even experienced users only access a portion of what these tools can do. “I think my entire vocabulary of Photoshop is probably 7% of its capabilities,” Huang said. That gap has always existed. What Huang describes is a way to close it. Systems that can understand intent make it possible to access more of a tool’s capability without needing to master every function manually. “We used to use tools by point and click and loading files and dragging down menus,” he said. In this model, the user does not need to navigate the tool step by step. They describe what they want and the system carries out the task by drawing on the full range of capabilities available in the software. Advertisement ## From interfaces to intent Huang described a change in the layer that sits in front of software. Applications remain in place, but the interaction model changes. “All the front end of SaaS is now agentic. It’s intelligent,” he said. This creates a different relationship between user and tool. Someone can still work directly inside applications like Photoshop or Premiere. At the same time, they can work through a system that interprets instructions and handles more of the execution. For companies, that has an immediate impact on how widely these tools are used. Functions that were previously hidden behind complexity become easier to access. “Tool use inside a company is skyrocketing,” Huang said. This does not remove the need for expertise, but it changes where that expertise applies. The advantage is no longer tied as tightly to knowing how to operate the software itself. It moves toward understanding what to ask for and how to guide the result. Huang suggested this opens the door for a much broader group of people to produce high-quality work. “For 99.9% of the creators in the world, this is going to elevate your art,” he said. That idea connects to a wider pattern across AI adoption. As access expands, more people are able to produce work that previously required specialized skill. The output improves and expectations rise alongside it. #### Want to go deeper?
