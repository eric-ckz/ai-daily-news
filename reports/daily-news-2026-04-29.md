   - 摘要：[Will Knight](/author/will-knight/) [Business](/category/business) # OpenAI Really Wants Codex to Shut Up About Goblins “Never talk about goblins, gremlins, raccoons, trolls, ogres, pigeons, or other animals or creatures unless it is absolutely and unambiguously relevant,” reads OpenAI’s coding agent instructions. Photograph: TriStar/Getty Images OpenAI has a goblin problem. Instructions designed to guide the behavior of the company’s latest model as it writes code have been [revealed to include](https://github.com/openai/codex/blob/main/codex-rs/models-manager/models.json#L55) a line, repeated several times, that specifically forbids it from randomly mentioning an assortment of mythical and real creatures. “Never talk about goblins, gremlins, raccoons, trolls, ogres, pigeons, or other animals or creatures unless it is absolutely and unambiguously relevant to the user’s query,” read instructions in Codex CLI, a command-line tool for using AI to generate code. It is unclear why [OpenAI](https://www.wired.com/tag/openai/) felt compelled to spell this out for [Codex](https://www.wired.com/story/plaintext-open-ai-codex/)—or indeed why its models might want to discuss goblins or pigeons in the first place. The company did not immediately respond to a request for comment. OpenAI’s newest model, GPT-5.5, was released with enhanced coding skills earlier this month. The company is [in a fierce race with rivals](https://www.wired.com/story/openai-codex-race-claude-code/), especially [Anthropic](https://www.wired.com/story/claude-code-success-anthropic-business-model/), to deliver cutting-edge AI, and coding has emerged as a killer capability. In response to a [post on X](https://x.com/arb8020/status/2048958391637401718) that highlighted the lines, however, some users claimed that OpenAI’s models occasionally become obsessed with goblins and other creatures when used to power [OpenClaw](https://www.wired.com/story/malevolent-ai-agent-openclaw-clawdbot/), a tool that lets AI take control of a computer and apps running on it in order to do useful things for users. “I was wondering why my claw suddenly became a goblin with codex 5.5,” one user [wrote](https://x.com/TaraViswanathan/status/2048992206711927100) on X. “Been using it a lot lately and it actually can't stop speaking of bugs as ‘gremlins’ and ‘goblins’ it's hilarious,” [posted](https://x.com/LeoMozoloa/status/2049082289116582386) another. The discovery quickly became its own meme, inspiring [AI-generated scenes](https://x.com/sterlingcrispin/status/2049235461755855134) of goblins in data centers, and [plug-ins for Codex](https://x.com/kitlangton/status/2049155108290650154) that put it in a playful “goblin mode.” AI models like GPT-5.5 are trained to predict the word—or code—that should follow a given prompt. These models have become so good at doing this that they appear to exhibit genuine intelligence. But their probabilistic nature means that they can sometimes behave in surprising ways.
