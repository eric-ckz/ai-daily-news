It is whether their operating model is ready to support one. AI will not fix fragmented workflows, inconsistent data or processes dependent on spreadsheets, inboxes and individual employee knowledge. In many cases, it simply makes those weaknesses more visible. That matters for agents, brokers, carriers, managing general agents (MGAs) and captive managers. All of them are under pressure to respond faster, process more submissions and give customers clearer answers. AI can help, but only once the underlying work is structured well enough for the technology to be reliable. ## **Start with the data problem, not the tool** Insurance information may sit across policy administration systems, rating tools, claims platforms, shared drives, email attachments and spreadsheets maintained by different teams. Customer names may be recorded differently from one system to the next. Policy data may be incomplete, and supporting documents can be hard to retrieve. When an organization cannot identify which data is current, complete and owned by a responsible team, it is not ready to feed that data into an AI workflow with confidence. This is not a reason to postpone modernization indefinitely; it is a reason to sequence it properly. Before selecting an AI use case, map the process you want to improve from start to finish. A submission intake process, for example, may involve broker emails, PDFs, missing-information requests, manual data entry, appetite checks (the initial screen for whether a submission fits the carrier's risk preferences) and assignment to an underwriter. AI may extract data from documents or flag missing fields. It will not resolve unclear underwriting rules, duplicate records or inconsistent hand-offs between teams. Pick one process with a clear business problem rather than launching a broad AI program. Submission triage, claims document intake, policy servicing requests and certificate generation are common examples. Establish a baseline: How long does the work take? How many people touch it?
