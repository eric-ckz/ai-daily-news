The security frameworks most organizations are using today were not built for this. AI agents require the identity governance of a workforce and the technical controls of software at the same time. A user typed a prompt, a model generated a response, and a human decided what to do with it. For the past few years, security concerns focused on chatbot failure modes: hallucinations, prompt injection, jailbreaks, data leakage. However, humans always served as the final checkpoint between AI output and real-world action.   Related:[Cyberattacks Intensify Pressure on Latin American Governments](/cyber-risk/cyberattacks-latin-american-governments) Today, the agentic era forces a rewrite of that model. AI systems now query databases, chain tools together, join datasets in unanticipated ways, and kick off downstream workflows, and they can do it without a human reviewing each step. The risk is no longer what a model might say. It's about what an agent can access and execute. And in most organizations, agents operate under service accounts with inherited privileges far broader than their actual task requires. This makes AI security less a software problem and increasingly an identity and access challenge. ## The Blind Spots in Agentic AI The risks compound as organizations interconnect AI agents, chaining systems together, calling each other's APIs, traversing environments, and operating under multiple identity contexts. Execution paths become non-deterministic. An agent authorized to summarize sales data might, through a sequence of tool calls, end up accessing systems and datasets it was never intended to reach. Guardrails around models don't address this. The risk isn't just what happens inside the model; it's what happens between systems, in the seams where one agent hands off to another, where permissions are inherited rather than explicitly granted, and where no single team has full visibility into the chain. This vulnerability exists partly by design. Because agentic AI paths are unpredictable, teams often grant broad access to avoid constraining the agent's ability to deliver results. The same flexibility that makes agents valuable is what makes them difficult to govern. Related:[Google's Vertex AI Is Over-Privileged. AI agents break both sides of that equation. Their access patterns are dynamic and context-dependent. Their execution paths are difficult to predict.
